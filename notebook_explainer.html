What Is This Notebook Trying To Do?

Imagine you work at a company that sells plumbing fixtures, ceiling fans, and tools on Amazon. Every day, thousands of customers leave reviews. Reading them all manually is impossible. This notebook builds an automatic classifier that reads a review and answers two questions:

  • What aspect of the product is the customer talking about? (Quality? Price? Installation?)

Think of a restaurant critic's review: "The pasta was overcooked but the service was excellent and the price was fair." A human instantly sees three topics (food, service, price) and their sentiments (bad, good, good). This notebook teaches a computer to do exactly that — for product reviews.

Step 4 — Converting Text to Numbers (TF-IDF)

Neural networks can't read words — they only understand numbers. TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency) is the translator. It turns each review into a row of 250 numbers, where each number represents the importance of a particular word or phrase in that review.

🎵
Analogy: A Music Equaliser

Think of TF-IDF like an audio equalizer with 250 sliders. Each slider represents a word. If a review contains the word "installation" a lot (but "installation" is rare across all reviews), that slider shoots up high. If it contains "good" (common everywhere), that slider stays low. The resulting "audio fingerprint" of 250 numbers uniquely describes the review's content.

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer

vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer(
    min_df=20,          # ignore words appearing in fewer than 20 reviews
    ngram_range=(1,4),  # consider single words AND phrases up to 4 words long
    max_features=250   # keep only the top 250 most informative features
)
X = vectorizer.transform(X)  # each review → a row of 250 numbers

Why n-grams up to 4?

Single words miss context. "not good" means the opposite of "good." By looking at phrases (n-grams) of up to 4 words, the model captures these subtle patterns. "easy to install" as a phrase is much more informative than the three words separately.

Step 5 — Building the Neural Network

The notebook builds a small but effective feed-forward neural network using Keras/TensorFlow. It takes the 250 TF-IDF numbers as input and outputs 12 probability scores — one for each label.

Neural Network Architecture

Input Layer 250 TF-IDF features → 64 neurons
▼ ReLU activation (decides which neurons "fire")
Dropout (20%) Randomly turns off 20% of neurons during training
▼ prevents overfitting
Hidden Layer 64 neurons → 64 neurons
▼ ReLU activation
Dropout (20%) Another regularisation layer
▼
Output Layer (Sigmoid) 12 neurons → 12 probabilities (0.0 → 1.0 each)
🧠
Analogy: A Team of Specialists

Imagine Layer 1 is a team of 64 junior analysts who each scan the review for different signals. They pass notes to Layer 2 (another 64 analysts), who look for patterns in the patterns. Finally, the output team of 12 specialists each give a verdict: "I think this review is about Installation — 87% confident."

Why Dropout?

Dropout randomly "switches off" 20% of neurons during each training pass. This stops the network from memorising the training data (overfitting). Analogy: It's like studying for an exam where 20% of your notes are randomly removed each time. You're forced to truly understand the material rather than memorise specific examples.

Why Sigmoid output?

Each label is independent — a review can simultaneously be about Quality AND Price AND be positive. Sigmoid outputs a number between 0 and 1 for each label independently (unlike Softmax, which forces labels to compete). A score > 0.5 means the label is predicted as present.

Early Stopping

Training stops automatically if the loss doesn't improve by 0.05 for 5 consecutive rounds (epochs). Analogy: Like stopping a workout when you're no longer gaining strength — no point burning energy for zero gain.

Log Loss — What Is It?

Log loss (cross-entropy) measures how confident and correct the model's probability predictions are. A perfect model has log loss = 0. The closer to 0, the better. Comparing train vs test log loss tells us if the model is overfitting (training loss much lower than test loss = memorising, not learning).

Key Takeaways

  • Multi-label classification — Unlike most classifiers that predict one category, this model predicts 12 labels simultaneously. A review can be about Quality AND Price AND be positive all at once.
  • TF-IDF is fast & interpretable — While modern transformer models (like BERT) would likely perform better, TF-IDF + shallow neural network is lightweight, fast to train, and still effective.
  • Cross-validation gives honest scores — Testing on data the model was trained on is cheating. 30 rounds of cross-validation gives a realistic picture of real-world accuracy.
  • Early stopping prevents waste — If the model isn't improving, training halts. This saves compute time and avoids overfitting to quirks in the training data.
